What is
blockchain?

A chain of blocks!

Uhhhh...

Ok, ok, think of a block as a file, as a spreadsheet. And what does this spreadsheet record? Transactions! Who sent money to whom.

Who? Who where?

Anywhere! In the money system you're used to, banks keep ledgers of this stuff. An important job, maintaining ledgers! Blockchain is a technology that gives us trustworthy ledgers without needing to trust a central authority, like a bank.

Ok, but why separate blocks? Why chop the ledger into pieces?

Right. So, blockchain brings to the digital realm something that wasn't native here before: Decentralized consensus. A system of record that's reliable and incorruptible, stored on many machines around the world, maintained by incentivized strangers. Chopping the ledger into blocks is part of the clever computer-sciencey way to incentivize them. For an approachable technical explanation, check out this great explainer video.

Ok, so now instead of a bank knowing where all my money goes, I'm supposed to trust a bunch of strangers?

You trust strangers to maintain the ledger, but you don't need to let them know who you are. You don't need to "open an account" in a blockchain system, you can just use some software to create a "wallet". And if someone else sends your wallet money, that transaction gets recorded in the blockchain. You can make as many wallets as you want; they look like a string of letters and numbers like "0x1ab9..." If you're careful, you can keep keep your wallet addresses separate from your real-life identity.
